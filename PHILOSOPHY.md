# Philosophy & Style Guide

This document is the measuring stick for every chapter in this repository. Read
it before writing or revising material. When a choice arises about *how* to
present something, this document decides.

## Who this is for

The reader is an **incoming 9th-grade honors student in the northeastern US**
who has spent several years in serious enrichment programs (Russian School of
Mathematics, Art of Problem Solving) and has competed in math competitions. They
are not a beginner. They are fluent in algebra, comfortable with abstraction for
their age, and — crucially — *already enjoy mathematics*. They do not need to be
coaxed; they need to be taken seriously.

Assume the reader is bright, curious, and impatient with hand-waving. Never talk
down. Never pad. If something is genuinely subtle, say so and slow down; if
something is routine, dispatch it briskly and move on.

## What this is for

1. **Summer review and preparation** for the year ahead.
2. **Long-term preparation for the AMC 10/12** and the olympiad pipeline beyond.

But the *purpose* is larger than either: to build genuine mathematical
understanding and taste. Competition success and course grades should fall out
as side effects of understanding the subject deeply, not the other way around.

## The standard of exposition

The bar is set by:

- Courant & Robbins, *What Is Mathematics?* (and Courant generally)
- Kolmogorov et al., *Topics in Elementary Mathematics* / the Russian tradition
- Art of Problem Solving publications

What these share — and what we are imitating:

- **Ideas before machinery.** A concept is motivated by a question worth asking
  before any formalism appears. The reader should feel *why* a definition is the
  right one, not just be handed it.
- **Connections across fields.** Algebra is shown talking to geometry; a
  counting argument reappears in a probability; the discriminant is the same
  idea as a perfect-square condition is the same idea as a tangency condition.
  These parallels are the whole point, not decoration.
- **Intuition is built explicitly.** We say what a result *means*, what picture
  to hold in your head, what to expect before computing. Then we make it
  rigorous. Intuition and rigor reinforce each other; neither is sacrificed.
- **Honesty about depth.** When a result has a deeper story (a glimpse of
  complex numbers, of symmetric functions, of a theorem they'll meet in a few
  years), we point at it honestly rather than pretending the elementary
  treatment is the whole truth. Foreshadowing is welcome.
- **The aesthetic dimension is real.** Elegance, surprise, and economy are
  treated as features worth noticing and naming. A beautiful proof is presented
  as beautiful.
- **History and people, used sparingly and well.** A historical note earns its
  place when it illuminates *why* a question was asked, not as trivia. Placing
  elementary mathematics in its historical context is encouraged.

## Tone

Cool, matter-of-fact, professional. State things plainly and let the mathematics
carry the interest. Avoid breathless superlatives ("irresistible," "remarkable,"
"a magic trick," "gloriously"), manufactured drama, and second-person
cheerleading ("take a moment to feel," "hold onto it"). Confidence reads as
restraint, not enthusiasm. The reader is bright; trust the material to land
without being told how to react to it.

## What this is NOT

- **Not a tutoring manual.** No "Step 1, Step 2, Step 3" recipe-following
  divorced from understanding. No teaching to a test.
- **Not a problem dump.** Problems are part of the exposition, chosen and
  sequenced to develop ideas — not a disconnected appendix.
- **Not a reference/textbook in the dry sense.** We are not cataloguing facts.
  Every section should be readable straight through as an essay with a thesis.
- **Not hand-wavy.** "It can be shown" is a last resort, used only when the
  argument is genuinely beyond reach, and then flagged as a debt.

## How a chapter should read

A chapter is an **essay with a thesis**, not a list of subsections. It should
have narrative momentum: each section answers a question raised by the previous
one. A reader should finish a chapter feeling they were *led somewhere*, not
handed an inventory.

Concretely:

- **Open with a real question**, not a definition. ("How high does a thrown ball
  go, and when?" beats "A quadratic function is...".)
- **Worked examples are arguments**, not templates. Show the thinking, including
  false starts when a false start is instructive.
- **Multiple representations.** Move deliberately among the symbolic, the
  geometric, the numerical. Make the translations between them explicit, because
  the translation *is* the mathematics.
- **Connective tissue.** Cross-link to other chapters with `[[ ]]`-style links
  or relative Markdown links. Reuse running examples across chapters where it
  reveals a parallel.
- **Starred / "Deeper" asides** (clearly marked) for the genuinely advanced
  glimpse, so the main thread stays clean but the curious reader is rewarded.

## How problems should work

- **Graded, and the gradation has meaning.** Warm-ups consolidate; standard
  problems require combining ideas; challenge problems are AMC-flavored or open
  the door to the next idea. A few problems should *teach* something not covered
  in the exposition.
- **Solutions teach.** A full solution explains the *choice* of method and why a
  natural first attempt does or doesn't work — not just a clean final derivation.
  Where instructive, give two solutions to show that perspective matters.
- **Competition flavor is woven in, not bolted on.** AMC-style problems appear
  where they naturally illustrate the topic, with the relevant technique named.

## A test for any passage

Ask: *Would Courant have left it like this?* If the passage merely states a true
fact correctly, that is not enough. It must also make the reader understand why
the fact is true, why it matters, and what it connects to.
